## Core Frazier principles

1. **Walls UP vs walls DOWN.** The first framing of the conversation determines everything. "Would you like to sell your business?" is walls-up — defensive, threatening, often angry. "I'm an investor looking for a fit" is walls-down — non-threatening, open, generic enough to invite a conversation.

2. **Position as an investor, not an acquirer.** Investment = provision of capital. Capital = resources, skills, knowledge — NOT just cash. You ARE a real investor because you bring the AI/tech modernization layer + the operator-grade systems thinking + (with Dan) the CPA-network access. This is authentic, not a positioning trick.

3. **Authenticity over interrogation.** People love to talk about themselves and their business. Your job in the first conversation is to listen + build rapport, not to extract data efficiently. Data comes in the second conversation.

4. **Don't ask for financials in the first ask.** "I need to see your financials and tax returns for the last three years" → they call their accountant/attorney → professionals get involved → NDAs appear → walls go up → you've added friction before establishing whether this is even a fit.

5. **Future-pace the seller into post-business life.** Get them excited about what they'll do AFTER the sale. This (a) makes them more emotionally bought into the transition and (b) reveals their actual minimum cash-at-close number, which often unlocks high-percentage seller-financing options.

6. **"Zero money out of pocket," not "zero down."** Never bring zero money to the table — find it from inside the company (cash on hand, AR), from third-party note-buyers (selling off the seller note at close), or from your own equity (Elena's cash). The seller almost never needs the full purchase price in cash at close.

## The Initial Outreach Message (Will's customized v2)

Use for direct seller outreach via cold email, LinkedIn, or letter:

> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm an investor looking for a bookkeeping / accounting firm in the Phoenix metro area doing between $200K and $500K in annual revenue. [Your firm] came up in my research and I wanted to reach out to see if you might be open to a conversation about a potential fit — either for an investment or some opportunity to work together.
>
> No specific agenda for the first conversation — just exploring whether there's mutual interest. Happy to do a 15-minute call when convenient.
>
> Best,
> Will

**Why this works:**
- "Investor" — non-threatening
- Specific region (Phoenix metro) + industry (bookkeeping/accounting) + revenue band ($500K–$1M) makes it concrete, not spam
- "Came up in my research" — credible (they were actually in the Apify scrape)
- "Either investment or some opportunity to work together" — leaves open: minority stake, partnership, full acquisition, advisory equity
- "15-minute call" — minimal commitment ask

**Don't customize-spam.** Each outreach should reference one concrete detail you actually researched (years in business, specialty, location specifics, recent press, etc.) — proves you're not blasting a list.

## First Conversation — Flow

When they respond YES to the outreach:

### Step 1 — Set the conversation up (don't drop a doc-request)

Open with:

> "Thanks so much for being open to chatting. I've got a few questions to ask to see how we might best work together. I'm going to ask for some basic information — sales and profits, a bit about the operations — as well as some softer questions about the company and you. Is this the best time to do that, or should we schedule a time when you can pull a few numbers together?"

DON'T send a financial-document request list ahead of time. That triggers the accountant/attorney involvement.

### Step 2 — Open with the story (rapport-first)

After they confirm they're ready to talk:

> "I'd love to hear the story of the company first — what's the history? How did it get started, how has it evolved, where is it now?"

Let them talk for 3–20 minutes. **Take notes — they're giving you priceless information.** While they talk, you're listening for:
- Common ground for rapport (alma mater, hometown, mutual contact, shared interest)
- Operational details that matter for evaluation
- Emotional cues about what they care about
- Pain points they reveal unprompted

When they mention something you genuinely connect with, name it specifically:
- *"I also went to [school]" / "My uncle did" / "I knew someone who"* — most specific first, then degrees of remove.

Don't fake connections — Will is honest, and lying erodes trust. Find real ones.

### Step 3 — Pivot to the operator

> "Thanks for that — really helpful. Tell me about you. What's your entrepreneurial journey? Is this the first business you've been in?"

Let them talk again. More rapport, more soft info. Often this is where you learn about other businesses they've owned, family ownership history, their personal motivations.

### Step 4 — Move to the data ask (conversationally, not as a form)

Now you can ask for the operational/financial info — but as conversation, not a checklist. Frazier's note: he has a "target information data sheet" he fills out as they talk. Same idea — you're capturing the info in your notes, but they're answering questions, not filling out a form.

**Topics to cover (don't lead with all of them; weave through naturally):**

**Operational:**
- Top-line annual sales (last 12 months, trend over past 3 years)
- Net profit / SDE / EBITDA (rough magnitude OK)
- Cash on the books
- Accounts receivable balance
- Accounts payable balance
- Long-term debt (SBA loans, equipment financing, owner loans)
- Real estate (owned vs leased — owned changes deal structure significantly)
- Machinery / FFE / equipment value
- Inventory (raw / WIP / finished) — usually minimal for bookkeeping firms but check
- Vehicles
- Number of employees · how many are managers / key client relationships

**Why employee count matters**: more employees → more likely to have manager-tier staff who can run the firm → more absentee-friendly. Aligns directly with the Codie-honest deal box requirement (Year 2 truly-absentee via 12-month manager-hire trigger).

**Strategic:**
- What's the owner's reason for thinking about selling / leaving?
- What's the client mix (concentration risk? top 5 clients = what % of revenue?)
- How do they get new clients (referral / inbound / outbound / paid)?
- What's the role of the owner day-to-day (key-man risk indicator)?

### Step 5 — Future-pace into post-business life

This is the highest-value question Frazier teaches:

> "If you sold the business, what would you do? What does life look like after this?"

Listen for: travel plans, family time, next venture, retirement, charitable work. Get them genuinely excited about that picture.

Then the follow-up that unlocks deal structure:

> "How much do you think that would cost, on a monthly basis?" — OR — "What number would you need from the sale to actually do that?"

**This reveals the minimum cash-at-close they actually need.** Often it's a small fraction of the headline asking price. If they're asking $1M but only need $100k to fund "travel with my husband," you've identified **90% seller-financing room** — your real cash-at-close is $100k, not $1M.

## After the First Conversation

End the call with a clear next step. Don't promise more than you'll deliver.

> "This has been really helpful — I appreciate you sharing all of this. Let me digest what you've shared and put together a few thoughts. Can I follow up by [specific day / 2–3 business days] to either schedule a follow-up conversation or share an initial offer for you to react to?"

Send a brief thank-you note same day. Within 2–3 days, do ONE of the following:
1. **Schedule a deeper conversation** (most common — there's more to learn)
2. **Send a "no-go" note** (if it's clearly not a fit — protect their time, protect your reputation)
3. **Send a soft LOI** (if it's clearly a fit and you have enough info)

## Anti-patterns to avoid (Frazier's hit list)

- ❌ "Do you want to sell your company?" as the opening — walls-up, often hostile response
- ❌ Asking for tax returns / financials in the first outreach — invites the lawyer/accountant gate
- ❌ Sending an NDA early — slows things down, signals you don't trust them, and you're not even sure this is a fit yet
- ❌ Faking commonalities to build rapport — they'll spot it; trust collapses
- ❌ Pretending you have a war chest of cash you don't have — be honest about being a "creative-structure investor"
- ❌ Cold-spamming the entire Apify list with the same message — quality > quantity; each outreach should reference one researched detail
- ❌ Asking for ALL info on the first call — let the conversation breathe
- ❌ Promising specific deal terms before you have enough info
- ❌ Talking more than you listen on the first call
