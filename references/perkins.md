## Core Thesis

**Your goal is not to maximize your wealth at death — it's to maximize your lifetime fulfillment.** Wealth at death is wasted life energy: every dollar you die with represents hours of work you spent converting your life into money you never used. The optimization is **convert money → experiences → memory dividends, before the window closes.**

Most people optimize for the wrong variable (net worth) because money is easier to measure than fulfillment. They die with hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars left over — not because they were generous (most heirs receive at age 60+, well past their highest-utility years), but because of **autopilot, fear of running out, and inability to think probabilistically about death.**

**Will's reframe:** retirement-target math (`$X by age 65`) is an autopilot trap if you don't also calculate when you stop having the energy/health to enjoy what the money buys. The whole *point* of accumulating is to spend it before the curve drops.

## The 9 Rules

| # | Rule | One-line operational summary |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Maximize positive life experiences** | Choose experiences deliberately; don't autopilot through life. Each experience pays a *memory dividend* — ongoing returns long after the event. |
| 2 | **Start investing in experiences early** | Memory dividends compound. An experience at 25 pays out for 60+ years; the same experience at 75 pays for 10. Front-load. |
| 3 | **Aim to die with zero** | Hitting *exactly* zero is impossible, but the target reorders your priorities away from over-accumulation. |
| 4 | **Use all available tools to die with zero** | Annuities, withdrawal-rate planning, deliberate spend-down, gifting before death. Money you don't spend by your end is wasted potential. |
| 5 | **Give to kids / charity when it has maximum impact** | The peak inheritance age in the US is ~60. If you have kids, transfer wealth at age 26-35 (their high-utility window), not at your death. Will: **no kids in the plan, so this rule simplifies to "give to charity early or skip entirely."** |
| 6 | **Don't live on autopilot** | Don't auto-save 20% forever because that's the rule of thumb. Don't keep working past your survival threshold just because that's what you've always done. Re-evaluate yearly. |
| 7 | **Time-bucket your life** | Group future years into 5-10 year buckets. Place experiences in the bucket where they'll yield the highest fulfillment (skiing in 30s, not 70s; family travel before kids leave, not after). |
| 8 | **Know your peak — stop growing wealth at the right time** | Net worth should peak around age 45-60 for most people, then *deliberately decline*. Past that peak, every additional dollar saved is a dollar you won't get to spend. |
| 9 | **Take biggest risks when you have little to lose** | 20s-30s = low downside (recoverable), high upside. Bold moves at 28 cost less than at 48. Quit a job to start something. Move countries. Risks deferred = risks not taken. |

## Key Concepts (applied to Will's situation)

### Memory Dividend
**Experiences pay ongoing emotional/psychological returns** — recall, retelling, identity formation — for years after the event itself. Material possessions depreciate fast; experiences appreciate. The math: an experience at age 28 with 60 years of memory dividend is structurally a better "investment" than the same experience at 78 with 10 years left to compound.

**Will + Elena application:** SoCal trip (Jun 19-21), NYC trip (Jul 30 - Aug 5), wedding/honeymoon (Oct 2027) all count. Re-do the math on the post-cut reverse-diet NYC trip: don't optimize the budget to the dollar — the memory dividend curve says spend a little more for the experience that compounds.

### Survival Threshold (the foundational formula)
This is the minimum wealth you'd need to **stop earning entirely from today onward** and still survive — *not* the goal, just the floor below which spending decisions are existentially constrained.

**Formula:** `Survival threshold ≈ 0.7 × annual cost of living × years left to live`

The `0.7` is Perkins's heuristic assuming ~3-4% real returns: you only need 70% of (total years × annual cost) because interest covers the rest. Higher real return → lower factor (5% real ≈ 0.58). Zero return → 1.0 (full amount needed).

**Critical distinction (the thing I got wrong on first draft):** the survival threshold is NOT the same as the coast number we use for retirement planning.
- **Coast number** ($266k today for $110k lifestyle at 6% real over 34 yrs to Elena 65) = enough invested today that the portfolio compounds to support retirement *starting at age 65* — **assumes you keep earning until 65**.
- **Survival threshold** = enough invested today that you can stop ALL income *right now* and live off the savings until death.

For young earners (Will 26, Elena 31), the survival threshold is huge because there are 60+ years to fund. The coast number is much smaller because the portfolio only has to cover post-retirement years.

Perkins's central provocation: **your net worth should peak between age 45 and 60 and then deliberately decline.** If it keeps climbing into your 60s/70s, you mathematically cannot die with zero.

**The peak should be a date you set deliberately, not a number you hit and accidentally drift past.**

### Time Buckets
**Place experiences in the life-decade where they yield maximum fulfillment.** Don't make a single bucket list — make ~5-10 buckets, fill each with appropriate experiences.

| Bucket | Typical fit | Will + Elena specific |
|---|---|---|
| **20s** (Will now) | High physical risk, low recovery cost, identity-forming, novel | NYC, SoCal, wedding ramp-up. **Apply Rule 9 — take bold career risks NOW** (acquisition, exit Amazon). |
| **30s** (Elena now, Will entering) | High earning, high mobility, peak experience years | Acquisition operating years; travel-heavy ; physical adventures (BJJ, Muay Thai resume); ambitious career bets |
| **40s** | Wealth-building peak, peak skill in chosen domain | Business at maturity; lifestyle upgrade; international travel cadence |
| **50s** | Net worth peak begins to taper, health still strong | Drawdown begins; "second-act" interests; big experiences while body cooperates |
| **60s** | Peak experience consumption + health glide | Frequent travel, second home territory, major bequests if any |
| **70s+** | Memory dividends from earlier buckets carry the experience load | Local, slower, lower-spend; portfolio actively draining toward zero |

### "What about the kids?" — N/A for you
Perkins spends a chapter dismantling the inheritance autopilot: most parents who say "I'm saving for the kids" haven't actually decided how much, when, or under what conditions. The average heir gets the money at age **60** — too late for the high-utility window of 26-35.

**Perkins's recommendation if you HAD kids:** decide the exact amount per kid (suggestion: ~$50k as a discretionary starter), give it at age 26-35, and zero out everything else through your own spending. Make the gift deliberate, before death, when it has the most utility.

**Will + Elena clarified 2026-06-07: no kids in the plan.** So this entire constraint drops away. The Die With Zero math is purer: no inheritance buffer needed, no generational wealth target, no college fund. **Spend it all on the two of you.** Charitable giving still applicable if values-aligned.

### "But I love my job"
Even if you love working, you should still die with zero. Loving your job means you might delay your peak (work longer), but the spending-down framework still applies — and the older you get, the less money buys in fulfillment terms. **Plan to die with zero anyway, even from a position of "I want to keep working."**

**Will application:** the late-2027 Amazon exit + acquisition path is a deliberate "I love my work but I love acquiring more" move. Don't let acquisition become a new "I love this job" autopilot where you optimize the business for another 20 years past peak utility. The business is a means to fund time + experiences, not a thing to maximize indefinitely.

## Annuities + longevity hedging

Perkins's pragmatic answer to "but what if I run out of money?": **buy an annuity at 55-65** that converts a lump sum into guaranteed lifetime monthly payouts. Math:
- $500k lump sum at age 60 → ~$2,400/mo for life
- Payout rate typically higher than 4% safe withdrawal (~5-6%)
- Removes longevity risk (the insurance company self-insures across many buyers)

**Will + Elena application:** worth flagging as a planning option for age ~60. If the portfolio is, say, $3M at that point and lifestyle is $100k, an annuity for $1-1.5M of it locks in income for life, freeing the remaining $1.5-2M for active spend-down. **Revisit in late 40s.**

## The honest tension with Schofield + Sanchez

| Framework | Says about money |
|---|---|
| **Schofield** (`references/schofield.md`) | Mostly silent on money — focused on training/nutrition |
| **Sanchez** (`references/sanchez.md`) | Maximize cash-flow, build wealth via acquisition, accumulate to scale |
| **Perkins** (this doc) | Stop accumulating earlier. Die with zero. Don't waste life on autopilot save. |

These are not in conflict — Sanchez tells you HOW to build wealth efficiently (the acquisition path); Perkins tells you WHEN to stop and WHY. **Use Sanchez to get the cash-flow engine running by age 30-35. Use Perkins to set the peak date around 50-55 and actively spend down past that.** The acquisition is a means, not an end.

If you ever feel the pull to "just one more deal" or "let me build the portfolio bigger first" past the peak date — that's the autopilot trap Perkins is yelling about. Use the `goals/elena-scale-back.md` decision as the first real test of whether you can hold the Die-With-Zero line when it requires foregoing real savings.
