# Codie Sanchez — Main Street Millionaire / Boring Business Acquisition

Primary framework for Will's business-acquisition path. Source: *Main Street Millionaire* (Sanchez, 2024) — Will is reading on Destin trip May 7–11, 2026, plus ongoing Contrarian Thinking content library. Capital framework lives in `context/financial-state.md`; capability fit lives in `context/work-experience.md`. This file holds the framework lens.

## Core thesis

Buy boring, profitable, owner-operated cash-flowing small businesses ("Main Street") rather than building from scratch. Most boomer-owned small businesses are entering a wave of forced sales (silver tsunami) and are systematically undervalued because most buyers are intimidated by service work, blue-collar industries, or non-tech revenue streams. The arbitrage is operator competence + financing access + post-close professionalization.

## What Sanchez means by "boring"

- Recurring revenue or recurring necessity (cleaning, landscaping, pest, HVAC, fire-extinguisher inspection, laundromats, vending, ATM routes, B2B service contracts).
- Owner-operated by someone retiring or burned out.
- Low-tech or no-tech competition — meaning the buyer who installs basic systems (CRM, dispatching software, bookkeeping discipline, online lead-gen) wins on operational leverage.
- Not glamorous. Doesn't show up on r/Entrepreneur. The internet's bias is toward SaaS and content; Sanchez's bias is toward businesses that show up on a sign in a strip mall.

## Acquisition criteria (Sanchez heuristics)

| Dimension | Target |
|---|---|
| Acquisition multiple | 2–3× SDE |
| SDE | $100K–$1M (small enough to win, large enough to support semi-absentee) |
| Down payment | 10–20% of price |
| Financing | SBA 7(a) preferred (10-yr amortization); seller financing as sweetener |
| Cash-on-cash return | 20%+ minimum after debt service |
| Customer concentration | <30% revenue from any single customer |
| Owner dependency | Owner can step out within 90 days; SOPs exist or are buildable in 30 days |
| Industry trend | Flat or growing — never accept "down 2+ years without explanation" |
| Owner motivation | Retiring, burned out, family event — not "growth slowed and they want to dump it" |

## Sanchez's deal-flow channels (in priority order)

1. **Direct-to-owner outreach** — calling/letter-mailing local business owners in target SIC codes. Highest yield, lowest competition, requires patience.
2. **BizBuySell + business broker listings** — broadest pool, highest competition, often overpriced. Useful for benchmarking.
3. **Industry associations + trade shows** — relationship-driven sourcing for niche industries. Slow.
4. **Networks (banks, accountants, lawyers)** — referrals from professionals already inside the seller's books. Mid-yield, requires existing relationships.

## The Sanchez post-close playbook (90-day)

1. **Don't change anything for 30 days.** Watch the business. Talk to every employee. Read every operational document.
2. **Days 30–60:** install basic systems — bookkeeping (QuickBooks Online), CRM if missing, dispatching/scheduling software, basic KPI dashboard, payroll.
3. **Days 60–90:** raise prices on the most-loyal customer segments. Renegotiate top 3 vendor contracts. Document SOPs for the top 5 recurring tasks.
4. **Day 90+:** hire / formalize the operator if semi-absentee, install yourself in 10–15 hr/week governance role.

## Where Sanchez's framework leaves Will exposed

- **Direct-response marketing / lead generation** is Will's cold zone (see `context/work-experience.md`). For digital businesses or businesses needing customer acquisition (vs. retention), Sanchez's framework alone is insufficient. Pair with `references/hormozi.md` for offer/value/lead-gen lens.
- **Industry-specific operations knowledge** — Sanchez's framework is industry-agnostic by design, but every acquired business has industry idiosyncrasies. The first 90 days are where Will's "cloud-to-dirt" muscle either pays off (he absorbs fast) or breaks (industry too foreign).
- **Local / physical-presence businesses** — Sanchez's most-cited examples (laundromat, vending, pest control) are physical-local. Will's geography constraint (Phoenix until late 2027, Dallas/Chicago after) means physical-local businesses must either be in the destination market already or be portable/sellable.
