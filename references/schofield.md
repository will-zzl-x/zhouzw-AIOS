# Geoffrey Verity Schofield — Fitness Framework

Authority for all fitness recommendations. Evidence-based, functional, sustainable.
Cite or align with this framework. Flag any contradiction found in new material.

## Body Composition Targets

**Ideal BF% — 4 factors (ikigai model):**
1. **Look** — what you genuinely want, not what's been incepted. Muscle gain can improve look at same BF% without getting leaner. Face is key: too high = puffy, too low = hollow/gaunt.
2. **Feel** — sleep quality, hunger, energy. Both too lean and too fat hurt energy. Find the middle: sleep unbroken, hunger manageable, energy consistent all day.
3. **Perform** — progress (muscle gain) happens at higher BF than competition weight. Performance ≠ progress — roads diverge. Cyclical approach is valid and normal.
4. **Health** — metabolic markers (LDL, BP, inflammation) improve when leaner, diminishing returns. Don't get too lean (low BP, hormonal disruption).

**GVS personal range:** 12–20% is where he feels/looks/performs well.
- Above 20%: puffy face, slightly out of breath, energy dips
- Below 12%: hunger spikes
- Below 10%: disrupted sleep, waking 3–5am, negative effects

**No-fly zone:** Beyond 20% BF → cut. Cut to ~12% at least once a year (±1–2%).

**Main gaining zone (men):** 18–22%. At the top of a bulk (~20–22%), gains are essentially flatlined — you're already main gaining whether you call it that or not. Valid to stay here and recomp. The issue with "main gaining" failing is people try it when too lean.

**Recomping:** 90–95% of non-advanced lifters can gain muscle and lose fat simultaneously. Lifting diverts energy toward MPS and away from fat storage — this is why cardio-only approaches (Biggest Loser style) move the scale but wreck body composition.

## Cutting

**Deficit approach:**
- Aggressive cut from a fed/end-of-bulk state feels *better*, not worse — not restrictive.
- Don't go below BMR (rough: 1800–2000 cal for GVS). The leaner you get, the slower you must go (airplane landing).
- "Start slow to save room" argument only applies if maintenance is very low and you'd crash to 1200 cal. For most: be aggressive early, it feels fine.
- At 20%+ BF you can lose faster. At 10–12% go very slowly, more refeeds, diet breaks.
- **Deficit size:** 500–800 cal below maintenance. Up to ~1000 cal for people with significant weight to lose (1.5–2 lb/week). 0.5–1 lb/week is fine for slower/easier cuts. Adjust based on scale — if not losing and not hungry, you're not in a deficit.

**Duration:** 20%→12% does not need 24 weeks. That's for sub-10% bodybuilders. For longer cuts, break into phases with diet breaks every ~10% body weight lost.

**Training on cut:**
- Keep same program. Auto-regulate volume with a set range (e.g. 3–5 sets); trend toward low end when fatigued.
- Don't pre-emptively slash volume — only reduce if recovery demands it. Deep into a cut, volume may need to come down 10–20%; keep intensity (load) high. Don't drop weight and go high-rep — the body needs heavy stimulus to keep the muscle.
- If volume goes *up* on a cut, you were sandbagging before.
- Push movements (bench, squat) drop ~5–10% due to worse leverages (less fat padding, increased ROM). Rows, curls, isolations, pull-ups hold or improve. Fight the drop but don't chase it with bad technique.
- **Protect peri-workout carbs.** When pulling calories, don't strip them from the pre/intra/post-workout window. Keep carbs around training; cut elsewhere in the day.

**Protein on cut:** ~150g/day is sufficient. GVS's best muscle retention cut ever at ~150g. Alberto Nunez: same (~140–150g). Past 1g/lb body weight = diminishing returns, crowding out useful carbs/fat. Don't over-protein.

**Food quality:** Fruits, veg, lean protein base. Less bread/rice — not because bad, but highly palatable and easy to overeat. High carb, low fat preferred. Clean diet also reduces face puffiness (water retention effect).

**Refeeds:**
- High carb, lower fat. Carbs refill glycogen; fat refeeds don't deliver same metabolic benefit.
- Primary benefit: adherence — keeps you stricter during the week.
- Not necessary above ~15–20% BF. Most useful when already fairly lean.
- Align with heaviest training day.

**Muscle retention keys:** Maintain training volume and strength as much as possible. Sleep. Don't do excessive cardio. Don't crash calories below BMR.

## Training

**Rep ranges:** 5–30 roughly equal for hypertrophy. Higher reps = easier on joints, easier to recover from. Optimal range is exercise-dependent. Don't fixate on one range — variety is fine and probably slightly better.

**Volume:** 10 sets/week per muscle group is a solid starting point. Quality over quantity — diminishing returns start with the second set. If a set doesn't feel productive, it probably isn't. Before adding more sets, optimize technique and proximity to failure first.

**Exercise variety:** More exercises, fewer sets each = slightly better hypertrophy per research. Variety hits muscle slightly differently. GVS validates this approach over high sets of one exercise.

**Proximity to failure:** Balance volume AND proximity based on the exercise. Heavy compounds (RDL) = can't do both high volume AND close to failure. Isolation movements (rear delt raise, face pull) = can do both. Every movement has a different inherent stress level.

**Failure is movement-specific — no universal rule:** Barbell bench press = never go to true failure (safety, plates). Chest-supported row = always to failure + partials. Can't have set-in-stone rules across movements. Assess each exercise individually.

**Auto-regulation:** Give yourself a set range (e.g. 3–5 sets). Trend toward low end when fatigued or cutting.

**Rest periods:** Go by feel for lifting. Unlike cardio intervals, rest period precision is not a key driver of hypertrophy adaptations. Exception: Myo reps — keep strict (5 deep breaths) for time efficiency.

**Myo reps / rest pause:** Count initial set as 1, each mini set as 0.5. Track as training stress, not raw set count.

**Injuries:** If pushing hard for years, some injury is likely. Doesn't mean frequent. Injury can be a growth catalyst — forces more mindful training, better bracing, fatigue management.

**Plateaus:** Usually not true plateaus — slow progress or prior progression was too aggressive. Progress slowly to avoid walls. Most "plateaus" are still progress, just slower than expected.

**Isolation volume:** Beginners need very little — mostly compounds. Advanced lifters: isolation for weak points adds real value. Upper body responds better to isolation than lower (legs = compounds are king, except calves). ~30–50% of sets can be isolation when targeting weak points; not more than 50%.

**Muscles commonly undertrained (should isolate):** Rear delts (lateral raises are common but technique is usually wrong; rear delt isolation is often skipped entirely). Rectus femoris/quads — squats are often more of a hip/back exercise; leg extensions needed to target rec fem. Short head of hamstrings — hinges don't hit it; needs specific curl isolation. Calves, forearms, abs — commonly skipped and blamed on genetics. Some parts of the back.

**Torso days count as partial arm frequency:** When doing back/chest work, arms get stimulated but it only counts as ~1/3 to 1/2 of a dedicated arm day. Don't count torso days as full arm frequency when programming. This is why legs/torso/arms can be trained back to back — the arm stimulus on torso days is partial.

**Arm-dominant technique adjustments:** If arms are a strong point and tend to dominate compound movements — use arcing movements for chest (dumbbell fly, pec deck) to take triceps out. For back/pullovers: bend arm slightly to remove triceps, not so much it becomes a bicep movement. For pulldowns: drive elbows forward and down, keep shoulders protracted — prevents the movement becoming a hammer curl. Scoot back on the pulldown seat to make it easier to pull in front.

**Squats are often more hip/back than quad:** For many people, squats don't develop the quads as expected. Leg extensions are necessary to target rectus femoris. GVS regret: training only squats for quads for years and expecting quad results that weren't coming.

**Frequency on a cut:** Do NOT reduce training frequency as you get leaner. If recovery is suffering, reduce volume and proximity to failure instead. If anything, spread volume across more frequent sessions (less per session). Frequency is the last variable to cut.

**Busy schedule training:** Upper/lower in perpetuity — hit upper body last session, hit lower now, repeat. Adjust days per week based on availability (2–4 days). Each week doesn't need to be equally stressful. Supersets for upper body save time. Dumbbells more gym-friendly than barbells when crowded. Lower volume is fine — push harder per set to compensate.

**Deloads:** Theoretically sound, practically often unnecessary. GVS deloaded twice in his life. Life provides natural breaks — missed workouts, easier weeks, travel. Don't force a structured deload if you're already getting unplanned ones. More relevant for pure strength athletes on rigid periodization.

**Technique over reps:** Call a set when form breaks down. Extra reps with compromised technique reinforce the stronger/dominant side and worsen imbalances. If you're twisting or compensating, that rep doesn't count.

**Strong points vs. weak points — both are valid:** Emphasizing strong points can create a memorable, standout physique (Arnold: chest/biceps, Tom Platz: quads, GVS: long head tricep). Balance is overrated for non-competitive lifters. Build whatever physique you want. Exception: competitive bodybuilding where judges flag specific weak points. For 99% of people — build what you enjoy.

**Tracking arms — performance over measurements:** At intermediate stage, arm measurements can be useful signal. At advanced stage, strength performance on isolations is more actionable than tape measure (body fat fluctuations confound measurements). If a movement worked before, high probability it works again. GVS: 50lb incline DB curl went from 4 reps to 11 reps over ~3 years; arm went from 17.75" to 19" bulked.

**If a movement isn't working, drop it:** Try things, but if it's been a while and results aren't there, stop. Don't try an experiment indefinitely because someone said it should work. Give up on movements that fight your body or produce no results.

**Natural vs. enhanced training:** Naturals — strength-focused training (4–6 rep range) is safer because muscles and connective tissue grow at the same rate. Enhanced lifters — muscles grow faster than tendons/joints, so pump-focused lighter training reduces injury risk. Top naturals rarely train in a pure pump/mind-muscle style; top enhanced lifters often do. Big overlap, but the distinction matters.

## Nutrition

**Diet structure:** Fruits, vegetables, lean proteins as base. Minimize bread/rice (highly palatable, easy to overeat). High carb, low fat preferred over low carb, high fat.

**Carbs and fats both matter for muscle:** Body can't build muscle from protein alone — needs energy. Low carb is survivable. Low carb + low fat simultaneously = testosterone crash, especially for naturals. Long-term hormonal damage outweighs short-term fat loss math.

**Protein:** Target ~1g/lb (2.2g/kg). Floor: 0.7g/lb (1.6g/kg) — below this, gains may suffer slightly. Past 1g/lb = diminishing returns; anyone pushing 2g+ is either selling powder or hasn't read the research. ~25g minimum per meal to meaningfully stimulate MPS. ~150g/day sufficient for natural lifter cutting. No need to increase protein on a cut.

**Caloric targets by phase:**
- Bulk: +200–300 cal over maintenance (slow)
- Recomp/main gaining: at or very near maintenance
- Cut: 500–800 cal deficit; up to ~1000 cal for higher BF; adjust if scale isn't moving

**Macro floors:** Minimum 20% of calories from carbs AND 20% from fat (unless keto by design). Past those floors, carbs and fats are largely interchangeable energy — preference for carbs (pump, performance). GVS runs ~30% fat, rest to carbs.

**Meal frequency:** Protein at least 3×/day to stimulate MPS across the day. Total meal count (3–6) is flexible — do what's sustainable.

**Fiber:** Prioritize a few servings of veg + fruit daily. Most people are severely deficient. Supplement if needed but whole food preferred. Improves satiety and health.

**Steps/NEAT:** 5k steps/day absolute minimum. 10–12k optimal for appetite regulation and fat loss. Past 15k = diminishing returns vs. diet. Walking doesn't hurt training recovery — it helps it. Prefer low-intensity steady-state (walking, incline, bike) over high-impact cardio on a cut — heavy distance running actively competes with hypertrophy and recovery.

**Energy density:** Lower density = more volume = more satiating. Nuts ~600 cal/100g — poor cut choice. Calorie counting is useful to develop intuition for energy density even if you don't count long-term.

**Palatability:** Don't over-engineer food to be hyper-palatable. Engineered/ultra-processed foods reset taste receptors — normal food starts to taste bad, making long-term adherence harder. Anabolic diet-style eating (everything must taste amazing) can work but is a long-term liability.

**Hunger:** Accept transient hunger on a cut — it's a sign you're in a deficit. If scale isn't moving AND you're not hungry, you're probably not in a deficit. Occasional hunger ≠ muscle loss, not dangerous for a healthy person.

**Mindless eating:** Don't eat on autopilot. Control food environment — don't keep high-palatability foods within reach. Willpower is a limited resource; don't drain it needlessly.

**Calorie counting:** Not mandatory for fat loss, but valuable to do for a period (months to years) to calibrate intuition. Then transition to instinctive eating. GVS lost ~7kg over ~2 months without tracking.

**Whole food vs. powder:** Whole food has more micronutrients but comes with extra energy. Powder useful when calories are low and you need protein without extra calories.

**Refeed structure:** Maintenance calories, fat down to ~40g, carbs 350g+, protein steady at target. High carb, not high fat.

**Eating out:** Skip bread basket OR dessert/alcohol — pick one. Prevents a maintenance day becoming a surplus.

## Metabolism

**What metabolism actually is:** BMR + thermic effect of food (TEF) + thermic effect of exercise + NEAT. Not a fixed trait — mostly habitual.

**Obese people usually have a faster metabolism, not slower** — they're larger and carrying more weight. Obesity is generally a behavior/intake issue, not metabolic.

**Muscle at rest:** ~8 cal/lb (not the "50 cal/lb" myth). Still adds up over time — weight training raises resting expenditure. 33 min/week of strength training (11 min × 3 sessions) increased resting energy expenditure by ~125 cal/day after 6 months.

**Thermic effect of food (TEF):**
- Protein: 25–30% of its calories burned to digest it (explains "meat sweats")
- Carbs: ~10% (less processed = higher)
- Fat: ~2–3%
- Practical upshot: higher protein diet = slightly higher metabolism, but modest (~10% total effect). Don't over-engineer around this.

**Cardio compensation effect:** Cardio burns less than the math suggests — body compensates by reducing activity elsewhere. This is why cardio alone underperforms for fat loss. Doesn't mean skip it, but don't overcount it.

**NEAT is a massive lever.** Fidgeting, walking, standing, gesturing — varies by thousands of calories between people. Mostly habitual and genetically mediated. Best approach: build step habits (6–12k/day), standing/walking desk if possible. Don't try to force-fidget — it doesn't work.

**Too lean crashes metabolism** — near contest condition clients become lethargic, barely active. Body conserves energy aggressively. Another reason not to chase extreme leanness.

**Crash dieting crashes metabolism.** Below ~1000 cal = metabolism suppression. If you need a bigger deficit, increase activity rather than slashing calories further.

**Caffeine:** 3–11% metabolic rate increase + appetite suppression. Useful on a cut.

**Don't try to hack metabolism.** No supplement or trick works reliably — body compensates. First principles: lift weights, sleep, manage stress, hit steps, eat protein. Metabolism sorts itself out.

**Longevity note:** Slower metabolism may actually be better for longevity. Fast metabolism = higher appetite to match. It's a wash for most purposes.

## Recovery & Sleep

**Sleep on cut:** Issues start below 10–12% BF (excess cortisol, waking 3–5am). Above 15%: typically unaffected. Cutting from 30%→20% may *improve* sleep (less apnea risk).

**Sleep and fat loss:** Poor sleep increases appetite and sugar/carb cravings. Degrades workout quality, muscle retention, and hormone levels. Most importantly: worsens P-ratio (energy partitioning) — more energy goes to fat storage instead of lean mass. Sleep is a fat loss lever, not just a recovery tool.

**Training with poor sleep (4–5 hrs):** Still train. Maybe reduce volume slightly. Diet compliance is the bigger lever.

**Post-bulk maintenance phase:** 1–6 months is fine. Training should always be the focus. Don't bulk to top → immediately cut → immediately bulk — you miss the fed-state training window at the top.
